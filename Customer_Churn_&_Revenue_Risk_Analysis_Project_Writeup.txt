OVERVIEW
--------
This project builds a complete end-to-end churn analytics pipeline for a
subscription-based business. Starting from raw customer, subscription, support,
and product usage data stored in a normalized MySQL database, I used SQL to
model and segment risk, Python to assign individual churn probability scores
using logistic regression, Tableau to build an interactive dashboard for
account managers and leadership, and Excel to deliver an executive summary
with a live what-if scenario model.

The dataset covers 20 customers across three segments — Enterprise, Mid-Market,
and SMB — generating $46,705 in total active monthly recurring revenue. Of the
20 customers, 8 have churned (40% overall churn rate) and 12 remain active.
The 8 churned customers represent $4,855 in lost monthly revenue.

The project answers three business-critical questions:
  1. Which customers are most likely to churn and why?
  2. How much revenue is currently at risk?
  3. What is the financial impact of reducing churn?


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 1 — DATABASE DESIGN
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

I designed a 4-table normalized MySQL schema to model all dimensions of the
customer relationship:

  customers
  ---------
  The central table. Stores account name, email, signup date, region, segment
  (Enterprise / Mid-Market / SMB), assigned account manager, and industry.
  20 accounts in total across 4 regions and 3 segments.

  subscriptions
  -------------
  One row per customer. Tracks plan name (Basic, Professional, Enterprise),
  monthly revenue, start and end date, contract type (Monthly vs Annual), and
  status (Active or Churned). This is the primary churn signal source.

  support_tickets
  ---------------
  All customer support interactions — category (Billing, Technical, Feature
  Request, Account), priority level, satisfaction score (1–5), and whether
  the ticket was resolved. 32 total tickets across the dataset.

  product_usage
  -------------
  Monthly behavioral data per customer. Tracks login count, number of distinct
  features used, reports exported, API calls, and a composite usage score
  from 0–100. 60 total rows — 20 customers × 3 months of data each.

The schema is fully normalized — no duplicated data, clear foreign keys from
every table back to customers, and clean join paths for all analysis queries.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 2 — SQL ANALYSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

I wrote 15+ queries organized into 5 sections, each feeding a different
downstream output (Python, Tableau, or Excel).

Section 1 — Revenue Overview
  Calculated MRR and ARR by segment and plan, quantified revenue lost to churn,
  and computed churn rate by segment. Key result: Enterprise had a 0% churn
  rate, Mid-Market churned at 57.1%, and SMB churned at 66.7%.

  MRR by plan (active customers only):
    Enterprise plan  — $37,500/mo across 7 customers ($450,000 ARR)
    Professional plan — $3,900/mo across 3 customers ($46,800 ARR)
    Basic plan       — $450/mo across 2 customers ($5,400 ARR)

Section 2 — Churn Risk Signals
  Identified the two strongest behavioral signals in the data:

  Signal 1 — Usage score collapse: Churned customers averaged a usage score
  of 13.1 in the months before churn. Active customers averaged 81.5. That
  is a 6x difference and the single clearest predictor in the entire dataset.

  Signal 2 — Billing tickets: Every churned customer had at least 3 billing
  tickets with satisfaction scores of 1.33–2.00. Every active customer had
  zero billing tickets.

  I built a risk classification query combining both signals into a single
  CRITICAL / HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW label per customer. All 8 churned customers
  classified as CRITICAL. 2 active SMB customers (QuickShip Couriers and Echo
  Marketing) classified as MEDIUM based on lower but not alarming usage scores.

Section 3 — Customer Health Score
  Designed a composite health score formula:
    health_score = (avg_usage_score × 0.5)
                 + (avg_satisfaction × 10 × 0.3)
                 + (min(tenure_years, 5) × 4 × 0.2)

  Usage score carries the most weight (50%) because it is the strongest
  predictor. Support satisfaction is secondary (30%). Tenure matters but
  has diminishing returns past 5 years (20%).

Section 4 — Usage Trend Analysis
  Compared average usage metrics between Active and Churned customers:

    Active customers:   avg usage score 81.5, avg logins 43.3/month
    Churned customers:  avg usage score 13.1, avg logins  5.8/month

  The gap is consistent across every behavioral metric — features used,
  API calls, and reports exported all show the same 5–8x difference.

Section 5 — Master Export
  A single comprehensive query joining all 4 tables into one row per customer
  with every feature needed for the Python model. Exported as churn_master.csv.
  This file feeds directly into the logistic regression model.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 3 — PYTHON CHURN MODEL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

File: Churn-Analysis_churn_model.py
Input: churn_master.csv (20 rows, 1 per customer)
Output: churn_scored.csv (same 20 rows + churn_probability + model_risk_label)

The script does 4 things:

  1. Loads churn_master.csv using pandas

  2. Selects 7 behavioral features as model inputs:
       avg_usage_score      — composite engagement metric (0–100)
       avg_logins           — monthly login frequency
       avg_features_used    — product breadth of use
       billing_tickets      — count of billing-related support tickets
       avg_satisfaction     — support satisfaction score (1–5)
       tenure_years         — length of customer relationship
       monthly_revenue      — contract value

  3. Scales all features using StandardScaler (required for logistic
     regression — ensures no single feature dominates due to scale
     differences, e.g. revenue $175–$7,200 vs usage score 0–100)

  4. Trains a logistic regression model and assigns a churn_probability
     between 0 and 1 to every customer

  5. Translates probability into a human-readable label:
       >= 0.75 → Critical
       >= 0.50 → High
       >= 0.25 → Medium
       < 0.25  → Low

  6. Exports churn_scored.csv for Tableau

Results from the model:
  All 8 churned customers scored Critical (probability range: 0.877–0.983)
  All 12 active customers scored Low (probability range: 0.005–0.127)
  The model cleanly separated churned from active with no misclassifications

The model's output confirmed the SQL analysis — the separation between
churned and active customers is so clear in the behavioral data that even
a simple logistic regression achieves near-perfect classification.

PART 4 — TABLEAU DASHBOARD
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

File: Churn Risk Dashboard.twb
Data source: churn_scored.csv

Built 4 sheets assembled into a single interactive dashboard:

  Sheet 1 — Revenue Overview
    Stacked bar chart of MRR by segment split by Active vs Churned status.
    Shows at a glance where revenue is concentrated (Enterprise, all active)
    and where churn has done the most damage (Mid-Market and SMB).

  Sheet 2 — Churn Risk Heatmap
    Segment × Region matrix colored by MRR. A filter control lets viewers
    drill into CRITICAL / HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW risk tiers. Clicking any cell
    filters all other charts on the dashboard simultaneously via a filter
    action — making the heatmap a triage tool for account managers.

  Sheet 3 — Customer Health Scatter (hero chart)
    Every customer plotted by avg_usage_score (X axis) vs churn_probability
    (Y axis), dot sized by monthly_revenue. Reference lines at usage = 40 and
    probability = 0.5 create 4 quadrants. All 8 churned customers cluster in
    the top-left danger zone (low usage, high probability). All 12 active
    Enterprise customers cluster in the bottom-right healthy zone.
    Hovering any dot shows customer name, segment, MRR, usage score,
    churn probability, and risk label.

  Sheet 4 — Usage by Risk Tier
    Horizontal bar chart showing average usage score per risk tier split by
    Active vs Churned status. A reference line at 40 marks the low-usage
    threshold. This chart explains the why behind the scatter plot —
    Critical tier customers have usage scores in the 10–19 range.

  Dashboard filter action: clicking a cell on the heatmap filters all
  4 charts to show only that segment and region combination.

KEY FINDINGS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. Usage score is the definitive predictor of churn. The gap between
   active (avg 81.5) and churned (avg 13.1) customers is so pronounced
   that usage score alone could classify most customers correctly. No
   churned customer had a usage score above 19. No active customer had
   a usage score below 47.

2. Billing tickets are 100% correlated with churn in this dataset.
   Every single churned customer had billing tickets with satisfaction
   scores of 2 or below. Every single active customer had zero billing
   tickets. A billing ticket with a low satisfaction score is the
   strongest early warning signal available to an account manager.

3. Contract type perfectly predicts retention. All 8 churned customers
   were on monthly contracts. All 7 annual contract customers (Enterprise)
   are still active. Monthly contract customers carry dramatically higher
   churn risk — likely because there is no switching cost or commitment
   anchoring them to the product.

4. Enterprise segment is completely healthy. 0% churn rate, all on annual
   contracts, all with usage scores above 82, zero billing tickets. The
   risk is entirely concentrated in Mid-Market (57.1% churn) and SMB
   (66.7% churn), both predominantly on monthly contracts.

5. The what-if model shows that retaining just 2 additional customers
   (reducing churn from 40% to 25%) recovers $93,410 in annual revenue.
   Cutting churn to 15% recovers $280,230 over 3 years — a number that
   justifies meaningful investment in customer success resources.

HOW TO REPRODUCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  1. Run Churn-Analysis_create_database.sql in MySQL Workbench
     (uncomment the first 2 lines before running)

  2. Run Section 5 of Churn-Analysis_queries.sql
     Export results as churn_master.csv

  3. Place churn_master.csv and churn_model.py in the same folder
     Run: python3 Churn-Analysis_churn_model.py
     Output: churn_scored.csv

  4. Open Tableau → connect to churn_scored.csv
     Open Churn Risk Dashboard.twb

  5. Open Churn_Executive_Summary.xlsx to view the executive summary
